Sharpening Dynamics With Non-Whitened Inputs

WORK IN PROGRESS This page is an in-progress companion to the main writeup. It relaxes the whitened-data assumption ($\Sigma_{xx} = I$) and is more exploratory than the first page: several results here are conjectural and flagged as such.

Where We Left Off

On the previous page every result rested on whitened inputs, $\Sigma_{xx} = I$. That single assumption did a lot of work: the gradient in function space pointed straight at the target, $\nabla_p\mathcal{L} = p - p^\star$, the preconditioner $M$ had $p$ as an eigenvector, and the sharpening region collapsed to the clean Thales sphere on the segment $[0, p^\star]$. The whole story reduced to "which side of a ball is the current function on."

Real data is not whitened. Even standard preprocessing (mean subtraction, per-feature scaling) leaves the input covariance with a nontrivial spectrum. Here we keep the same architecture — first layer $w_1 \in \mathbb{R}^d$, scalar layers $b_1, \dots, b_n$, effective map $p = B\,w_1$ — but let $\Sigma_{xx}$ be a general symmetric positive-definite matrix. The question is how much of the whitened picture survives.

What Changes, What Is Preserved

The preconditioner is a property of the parameters alone, so it is untouched by the data covariance:

$$ M = G G^\top = B^2\, I_d + B^2 S\, w_1 w_1^\top, \qquad S = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{b_i^2}. $$

What changes is the gradient. With general inputs,

$$ \nabla_p \mathcal{L} = \Sigma_{xx}\,(p - p^\star), \qquad p^\star = \Sigma_{xx}^{-1}\Sigma_{yx}^\top, $$

so the function flow is

$$ \dot p = -\,M\,\Sigma_{xx}\,(p - p^\star). $$

Two things break at once. The pull $-\Sigma_{xx}(p - p^\star)$ no longer points straight at the target — it is bent by the covariance — and the operator that sets the sharpness is now the non-symmetric product $M\,\Sigma_{xx}$ rather than $M$ alone. The sharpness is still the top eigenvalue of $M\Sigma_{xx}$ (its nonzero spectrum equals that of the Gauss–Newton matrix), but $p$ is in general no longer an eigenvector of the operator that drives the norm.

What is preserved: the radial bias of $M$ (it still amplifies motion along $p$), the conservation laws $C_i = \|w_1\|^2 - b_i^2$, and the direct map from function-space evolution back to weights. The geometry is deformed, not destroyed.

The Sharpening Boundary Deforms

In the whitened case the boundary between sharpening and flattening was $p^\top(p - p^\star) = 0$, a sphere. The same calculation with general $\Sigma_{xx}$ replaces the rate of norm change by

$$ \tfrac{1}{2}\frac{d}{dt}\|p\|^2 = p^\top \dot p = -\,p^\top M\,\Sigma_{xx}\,(p - p^\star). $$

The sign is no longer controlled by a single inner product, because $M$ and $\Sigma_{xx}$ do not share eigenvectors in general. Writing the boundary as an implicit surface $F(r, \theta) = 0$ — with $r = \|p\|/\|p^\star\|$ the norm ratio and $\theta$ the angle to the target — the whitened factorization $\cos\theta = r$ acquires an angular correction term that depends on the anisotropy of $\Sigma_{xx}$. The Thales sphere becomes an ellipsoid-like region whose shape is set by the covariance spectrum, and the clean factorization into "norm" and "angle" no longer holds exactly.

Open Threads

The following are active and not yet settled. They are listed here so the page doubles as a working agenda.

A theorem near initialization

Near small $r$ with imbalance $C > 0$, the top eigenvalue behaves like $\mu_+ \approx C(\lambda_1\cos^2\theta + \lambda_2\sin^2\theta)$, where $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ are the covariance eigenvalues. Whether sharpening actually occurs near initialization then hinges on the sign of $\dot\theta$ — the rate at which the angle to the target changes. Deriving $\dot\theta$ at small $r$ would convert the current conditional statement ("sharpens if the angle does not run away first") into a genuine theorem for non-whitened inputs.

Is this a mirror flow?

The preconditioned flow $\dot p = -M(p)\,\nabla_p\mathcal{L}$ looks like it might be a mirror flow for some potential. Settling this requires checking the integrability condition on $M^{-1}$: if $M^{-1}$ is a Hessian map, the flow is exactly mirror descent and we inherit that literature's implicit-bias results; if not, it is only "closely related." The verification is concrete (symmetry of the relevant mixed partials) but not yet done, so the framing stays cautious for now.

Can the non-whitened flow eventually flatten?

On the whitened page we proved a model that enters the sharpening region can never leave ($\dot g \ge 0$ on the boundary, with the leftover term $\lambda_\perp\|p^\star_\perp\|^2 \ge 0$). That proof leaned on $Mp = \lambda_\parallel p$, which fails when $\Sigma_{xx} \ne I$. So multiple sharpening/flattening phases become possible in principle, and the anisotropy condition for them requires sufficiently anisotropic (not merely nontrivial) data. Before claiming this in the writeup, it should be confirmed by explicit numerical integration — a $d = 3$ run with a deliberately ill-conditioned $\Sigma_{xx}$ is the cleanest test.

Connecting to dataset-difficulty bounds

It is worth checking whether the dataset-difficulty bounds in the recent literature (arXiv 2506.06940) reduce to covariance-spectral quantities in the whitened limit. If they do, the non-whitened analysis here and those bounds are describing the same object from two directions, which would be a useful cross-check rather than a coincidence.

A Note On "Normalization" vs. Whitening

It is tempting to wave away $\Sigma_{xx}$ on the grounds that we always normalize inputs. But standard preprocessing — for MNIST and similar — is per-feature normalization, not whitening: it rescales coordinates without decorrelating them, so $\Sigma_{xx}$ keeps a nontrivial off-diagonal structure and a spread spectrum. The $\Sigma_{xx}$ factors in every expression on this page therefore carry genuine meaning and cannot be dropped. This is precisely why the non-whitened case is the one that connects to practice.
